For the Daily Knoxville Dispatch Biographical A further notice of the the Dunlop Family in Tennessee The Ancestor of the Tennessee Dunlops was Hugh Dunlop, Esq. It is believed that he was born in Ireland. He was one of the earliest emigrants to Washington County. His brother Ephraim Dunlop was Prosecuting Attorney & did not fail to inflict the full penalty of the law against all opponents -- & particularly against the few Loyalists on that Frontier. The other brother Hugh moved along with the remaining population that lower down the county & reached at length the brut know known as Luchahn on the present county of Tenn. There he erected a cornmill -- the remains of which can still be seen on the Limestone Fork of the Creek. This mill encouraged immigrants to settle lower down the French-Broad. Amongst these immigrants was Donovan Gilliam It is believed W.G. came from Virginia. He was a gentleman of some considerable means & of more than ordinary endtion & attainments. this Grant contained above 800 Acres intending from a stone on the north Bank of French Broad County poles above its confluence with the Territory. The stone marked with the initials D.G. can still be seen at low ivertion mark. On this large ground Mr. Gilliam about 1785 erected his dwelling house -- a larger structure more tastefully finished thee was common on the Frontier -- it had a shingled roof & a large stone chimney -- taken from Marble- Vanny near to it. His house was afterwards sometimes used by his neighbors a s a Fort during the Cherokee Troubles & was known as Gilliam Station at the first settlement of the county. Mr. Gilliam was intelligent, hospitably kind to all & especially to strangers, enterprising & successful. Mrs. Gilliam was a superior woman in every aspect.
